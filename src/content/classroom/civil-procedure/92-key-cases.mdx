    <h3 className="text-lg font-semibold text-foreground">Erie Railroad Co. v. Tompkins (1938)</h3>
    <p className="mt-3 text-sm text-foreground/90">Federal courts sitting in diversity must apply state substantive law rather than general federal common law.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-foreground/90">The Erie doctrine ensures vertical uniformity by requiring federal courts to follow state substantive rules while applying federal procedural law.</p>

    <h3 className="text-lg font-semibold text-foreground">Hanna v. Plumer (1965)</h3>
    <p className="mt-3 text-sm text-foreground/90">When a valid Federal Rule of Civil Procedure conflicts with state law in diversity cases, the federal rule governs.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-foreground/90">The Rules Enabling Act authorizes application of procedural federal rules that regulate practice without abridging substantive rights, superseding contrary state procedure.</p>

    <h3 className="text-lg font-semibold text-foreground">Celotex Corp. v. Catrett (1986)</h3>
    <p className="mt-3 text-sm text-foreground/90">A party moving for summary judgment may point to the absence of evidence on an essential element, shifting the burden to the nonmovant to produce admissible proof.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-foreground/90">Rule 56 allows judgment when no genuine dispute of material fact exists; after Celotex, movants may rely on evidentiary gaps to demonstrate entitlement to judgment as a matter of law.</p>
