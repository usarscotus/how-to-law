    <h3 className="text-lg font-semibold text-foreground">Mullaney v. Wilbur (1975)</h3>
    <p className="mt-3 text-sm text-foreground/90">Maine violated due process by shifting to the defendant the burden of proving he acted in the heat of passion to reduce murder to manslaughter.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-foreground/90">The prosecution must prove beyond a reasonable doubt every fact necessary to constitute the crime, including the absence of heat of passion when it is part of the statutory offense.</p>

    <p className="mt-3 text-sm text-foreground/90">New York could require defendants to prove the affirmative defense of extreme emotional disturbance without violating due process.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-foreground/90">States may place the burden of persuasion on defendants for true affirmative defenses so long as the prosecution still proves the statutory elements beyond a reasonable doubt.</p>
