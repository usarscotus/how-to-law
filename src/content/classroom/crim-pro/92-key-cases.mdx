    <p className="mt-3 text-sm text-foreground/90">Electronic surveillance of a phone booth without a warrant violated the Fourth Amendment because the defendant expected privacy in his conversation.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-foreground/90">The Fourth Amendment protects people, not places, guarding reasonable expectations of privacy and requiring warrants absent a recognized exception.</p>

    <p className="mt-3 text-sm text-foreground/90">An officer may briefly detain and frisk a suspect based on reasonable suspicion that criminal activity is afoot and the person is armed.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-foreground/90">Stop-and-frisk encounters require articulable, specific facts supporting suspicion; the frisk must be limited to a protective pat-down for weapons.</p>

    <h3 className="text-lg font-semibold text-foreground">Miranda v. Arizona (1966)</h3>
    <p className="mt-3 text-sm text-foreground/90">Statements from custodial interrogation are inadmissible unless police advise suspects of their rights to remain silent and to counsel.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-foreground/90">Custodial questioning triggers procedural safeguards that require warnings and a voluntary, knowing waiver before statements may be used in the prosecution's case-in-chief.</p>

    <h3 className="text-lg font-semibold text-foreground">Gideon v. Wainwright (1963)</h3>
    <p className="mt-3 text-sm text-foreground/90">The Sixth Amendment requires states to provide counsel to indigent defendants charged with serious crimes.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-foreground/90">In felony prosecutions, the right to counsel is fundamental and binding on the states through the Fourteenth Amendment, obligating appointment of attorneys for defendants who cannot afford one.</p>
